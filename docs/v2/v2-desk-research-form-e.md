## 1. Form E — the practitioner reality

### Structure

Form E is a 28–30 page sworn financial statement prescribed under the Family Procedure Rules 2010, divided into 5 parts:

1. **General information** — marriage details, children, living arrangements, health issues, current maintenance
2. **Financial details** — all capital assets, liabilities, pensions, income (the bulk of the form)
3. **Income needs / expenditure budget** — detailed monthly outgoings
4. **Narrative sections** — contributions, standard of living, any other circumstances (Section 25 factors)
5. **Statement of Truth** — must be signed and sworn/affirmed. Lying is perjury.

### Typical solicitor workflow

Solicitors send the client a document checklist at the outset. The client gathers documents over 2–4 weeks. The solicitor drafts/populates the form, reviews with the client, and finalises. Incomplete client responses are the primary cost driver — each round of chasing missing items adds cost.

- **Professional time:** 5–15 hours depending on complexity
- **Client document-gathering time:** 20–30 hours spread over 2–4 weeks
- **Deadline:** Form E must be served no later than 35 days before the First Appointment hearing

### Section 2 deep dive — financial details

| Section | What it covers | Required documents | Common mistakes |
|---------|---------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **2.1** Property (family home) | Value, mortgage, equity, ownership share | Mortgage redemption statement (not just balance), 3 estate agent valuations or RICS surveyor | Using insurance value not market value; outdated valuations |
| **2.2** Other property | Buy-to-lets, holiday homes, land, overseas | Same as 2.1 per property | Omitting overseas property; forgetting beneficial interests |
| **2.3** Bank accounts | Every account held in last 12 months, sole or joint, including dormant, app-based (Monzo, Revolut), zero-balance | 12 months statements per account | Omitting accounts with small balances; forgetting closed accounts |
| **2.4** Investments | ISAs, shares, bonds, unit trusts, crypto, premium bonds | Latest statement per investment | Not including crypto; confusing premium bond face value with prize winnings |
| **2.5** Life insurance | All policies including endowments, critical illness | Surrender value statement (not sum assured) | Confusing death benefit with surrender value |
| **2.6–2.9** Other assets | Money owed to you, cash, personal belongings (>£500 second-hand), assets abroad | Varies | Using insurance/replacement value instead of second-hand value |
| **2.10** Business interests | Shares in private companies, partnerships, sole trader | Business accounts (2 years), valuations, director's loan account statements | Undervaluing goodwill; not disclosing DLA balances; failing to account for retained profits |
| **2.11** Directorships | Any directorship in last 12 months | Company records | Omission |
| **2.13** Pensions | All pension schemes with CETVs | CETV statement (within 12 months) per scheme | Using old valuations; confusing annual income with transfer value; missing small historic pots |
| **2.14** Liabilities | All debts: credit cards, loans, car finance, overdrafts, HMRC, student loans, informal loans | 12 months credit card statements, loan agreements | Only listing balances without rates/terms; omitting family loans |
| **2.15–2.20** Income | Employment (gross/net), self-employment, investment, rental, benefits, maintenance | Last 3 payslips (or 12 months if variable), P60, last 2 years tax returns if self-employed | Income sections described as "unworkable" by practitioners — income can appear in multiple sub-sections |
| **3.1** Expenditure | Detailed monthly budget across 15+ categories | Bank statements cross-referenced | Inflated budgets undermine credibility; understated budgets result in inadequate provision |

### Part 4 — Standard of living

Section 25(2)(c) MCA 1973. Courts want a narrative description of the kind of lifestyle: type of housing, holidays, schools, cars, clubs, domestic help, dining habits. Concrete specifics beat generalities. "Two foreign holidays per year (skiing in winter, Mediterranean in summer)" is better than "comfortable lifestyle."

**Common mistake:** Over-inflating (aspirational rather than actual), under-stating (backfires if contradicted), or vagueness ("we had a good standard of living" — gives the court nothing to work with).

## 2. D81 vs Form E

**Form E** — used during contested proceedings or early-stage disclosure for negotiations/mediation. Comprehensive financial snapshot.

**Form D81** — used when parties have already agreed and are asking the court to approve a consent order. Compulsory when submitting a consent order.

The new D81 (revised March 2022) expanded from 6 to 23 pages. It now requires:
- Full asset and income tables for both parties showing current positions
- A second set showing post-settlement positions
- Explanation of where each party and children will live
- **Section 10 — Reasons for Departure from Equality** (the most common rejection reason when left blank)

**Rejection rates:** No official statistics exist, but practitioners confirm a "large proportion" of D81 applications are returned because Section 10 is inadequately completed. More common with self-represented litigants. Courts typically request amendments rather than outright rejection.

**Court processing time for consent orders:** 6–10 weeks from submission.

## 3. Financial Remedies Court reform (2024–2026)

### Express Financial Remedy Procedure Pilot (PD 36ZH)

Running 7 April 2025 to 3 April 2026, applying by default in 33+ courts across northern England. Applies where net capital assets excluding pensions are below £250,000.

Key changes:
- **Compressed two-hearing structure:** First hearing (treated as FDR) at 16–20 weeks; final hearing at 26–30 weeks
- **Front-loaded disclosure:** Form E, valuations, mortgage capacity evidence, questionnaire replies, and settlement offers must all be filed before the first hearing
- Either party can request transfer to the standard procedure

**Implication for Decouple:** The Express Pilot front-loads the work. Users need their financial picture built faster. V2 directly serves this need.

### Digital submission mandate

From 24 August 2020, consent order applications must be lodged online via MyHMCTS. From 31 January 2023, legal representatives must submit contested applications digitally too. All uploads are PDF.

### Transparency Reporting Pilot

Extended to 29 January 2027 covering all financial remedy proceedings in England and Wales. Accredited media may attend and report (except FDR hearings), subject to strict anonymity. Property addresses, employer names, account details, and children's identities cannot be reported.

## 4. NFM Financial Mediation Pack

National Family Mediation provides a bespoke financial disclosure pack written in plain language, mapping directly to Form E sections. During mediation, the mediator displays all figures on screen for both parties to work on together.

**Output:** An "Open Financial Statement" (OFS) — explicitly not confidential, classified as "open information" usable in court. If parties agree, the OFS accompanies the consent order and D81 submission.

**Court acceptance:** Recognised as full disclosure for out-of-court resolution. If matters become contested, the court will likely still order formal Form E exchange.

## 5. Real user pain points

### "How the frig do I do the Form E?"

This Mumsnet thread title captures the universal initial reaction. Form E is described as "dreaded" by both users and solicitor firms. Users report feeling "sick, stressed and overwhelmed" when first looking at it.

On Mumsnet, one user asked about "Form E completion — pay someone to do it?" wanting someone to handle it for under £1,000, noting their solicitor would charge that for what is essentially administrative work.

### Pension CETVs are the single biggest bottleneck

On Mumsnet's "NHS pension CETV request for Form E — how long???" thread:
- One person's NHS CETV took **9 months**, plus 4 months for the pension report
- Another waited **11 months**
- NHS BSA told one poster it was "currently taking much, much longer, with a timeframe that could potentially be a year"
- Teachers' Pension CETVs: backlog peaked at 3,062 cases in October 2024. Some took over 12 months. Legal action launched by Leigh Day.
- The McCloud remedy (addressing age discrimination for 2015–2022 service) requires two calculations per member, compounding delays

Users on the "CETV — how long did you wait" thread shared timelines ranging from 6 weeks (private sector) to 12+ months (public sector).

### Pensions are incomprehensible to users

"I don't understand pensions at all" — Mumsnet user on "Pension CETV of 35,000 — what does this mean?"

Another user on "E Form — Pension Order — what do I say?" described comparing different pension types as "apples and pears." Professional guidance confirms pensions are "far too complex to be summarised in the 5 relevant boxes provided in part 2.13 of Form E."

### Document gathering is overwhelming

Users must provide 12 months of bank statements for every account, P60s, P11Ds, last three payslips, two years of tax returns if self-employed, business accounts, mortgage statements, and valuations for every asset worth over £500. Users on Mumsnet reported being surprised by the sheer scope.

### Non-disclosure is pervasive

- "Advice please — ex ignoring me & refusing financial disclosure" — ex described as "stonewalling" for months
- "Ex won't supply correct information for form E" — ex described as "a nightmare" putting incorrect details on the form
- "H not submitting form E" — husband simply refused to submit
- "String of lies on Form E" — lies about bonuses, possessions, job security, cohabitation status

Users described the process when someone refuses as predictable but slow: Form A → Form E order → first appointment → questionnaire → penal notice → contempt. "Has always got there in the end" but stretches cases from months into years. Described as "a form of financial abuse."

### Hidden assets

- "Hiding money" — concern about money concealed through a limited company
- "Evidence of hidden money" — ex "finally admitted hiding 30k"
- "Soon-to-be ex has 'hidden' all assets in Children's Savings" — £80k of equity placed in children's accounts, ex claimed it "didn't have to be declared as it belonged to the children" (users confirmed this is illegal)

Common tactics: transferring to undisclosed accounts, fake invoices, undervaluing assets, using company structures, overstating debts, placing assets in relatives' names.

### Self-representation is surging

96% increase in unrepresented family law cases between 2016 and 2023. Driven by cost — legal aid for divorce requires evidence of domestic violence or child abuse.

Users on "Becoming litigant in person" discussed switching from solicitor to self-representation mid-case. Professional guidance warns this often extends timelines and can damage settlements — what seems like a saving costs more long-term.

Advicenow's free Form E guides are described as "brilliant" across multiple Mumsnet threads. Form E is called "the one form you really do need to get right" when self-representing.

### What people wish existed

No forum posts explicitly request a "Form E app" by name. But the unmet needs are clear:

1. **"Pay someone to do it"** impulse — users see Form E as administrative burden they would outsource if affordable
2. **D81 preference** — users noted D81 is simpler and questioned why it couldn't be used more widely
3. **Advicenow's popularity** — proves hunger for plain-English guidance through the process
4. **Amicable markets around simplifying disclosure** — validates the pain point
5. **Mediate UK notes** "most people in mediation use their own disclosure forms rather than the lengthy official form"

The implicit wish across hundreds of posts: something that tells you what documents you need upfront, guides you through each section in plain English, tracks what's gathered and what's outstanding, handles the pension CETV bottleneck, automates the arithmetic, and costs dramatically less than a solicitor.

### The cost of financial disclosure

| Item | Typical cost |
|------|-------------|
| Solicitor help with Form E | £750–£1,500+ VAT |
| Court application (Form A) | ~£300 |
| PODE pension report | £1,900+ VAT (typically split) |
| Chartered surveyor valuation | £750+ VAT |
| Total to First Appointment | £5,000–£10,000 per party |
| First Appointment to FDR | Additional £10,000–£15,000 |
| Contested to final hearing | £30,000+ VAT |

One case cited: a client spent £12,000 on solicitor fees "just trying to get a Form E completed, with no progress on the finances or children's arrangements."

### Consequences of Form E errors

- **Cost orders:** £10,000–£30,000+
- **Adverse inferences:** Courts assume hidden assets with generous valuations favouring the compliant party
- **Settlement set-asides**
- **Contempt proceedings:** Fines £500–£5,000, imprisonment 14 days to 6 months
- **Fraud Act 2006:** Maximum 10-year sentence
- **Brown v Brown [2024]:** 19-day custodial sentence for persistent non-disclosure

## 6. Key findings — what V2 must get right

1. **Form E is administrative pain, not intellectual challenge.** Users don't struggle with understanding what to disclose — they struggle with the volume of document gathering, the complexity of the form's structure, and the cost of professional help. V2 should eliminate the administrative burden.

2. **Pensions are the #1 confusion and the #1 bottleneck.** CETV requests take months. The value is often misleading. Users don't understand what a CETV means. V2 must provide pension education, CETV tracking, and clear guidance on when a PODE is needed.

3. **The 96% increase in self-representation is our market.** These people need Form E help the most and can afford professional help the least. V2 is the tool they're wishing for.

4. **Non-disclosure by the other party is common and emotionally devastating.** While V2 focuses on the applicant's own disclosure, the product should acknowledge this reality and prepare users for it.

5. **The Express Financial Remedy Pilot front-loads everything.** Users in 33+ courts need their financial picture built before the first hearing at 16–20 weeks. Speed matters.

6. **Digital submission is already mandated.** Courts accept PDF. V2's Summary tab / export should produce court-ready output.

7. **"The dreaded Form E"** is a brand opportunity. The product that makes Form E feel manageable — that replaces dread with control — wins the market.
