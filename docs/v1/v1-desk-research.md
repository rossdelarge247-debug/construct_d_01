## 1. Cross-cutting findings

### The process is fragmented and nobody joins it up

Separation involves at least three separate legal processes (divorce, financial order, child arrangements) plus supporting processes (MIAM, mediation, child maintenance, benefit changes, housing). These are spread across GOV.UK (multiple sections), Cafcass, MoneyHelper, Citizens Advice, FMC, and the CMS. No official source presents a coherent unified journey.

### The "consent order gap" is the most dangerous knowledge gap

GOV.UK does not clearly explain that:
- Divorce and financial settlement are SEPARATE legal processes
- Finalising divorce without a financial order leaves financial claims open indefinitely
- Wyatt v Vince (2015): successful financial claim 20 years after divorce
- A consent order requires a professionally drafted legal document, not just the D81 form

### "It's not one process but three" is the #1 confusion

Users think "divorce" is one thing. In reality: (1) the divorce itself (ending the marriage), (2) financial settlement (dividing assets), (3) child arrangements. The divorce application takes 10 minutes on GOV.UK. The financial settlement can take years.

### No existing source provides triage or personalisation

Every source provides the same information to everyone. No mechanism helps a user work out: "My situation is X, therefore I need to do Y."

### Timeline and sequencing is never clearly communicated

The parallel nature of the process — that you should be doing financial disclosure, attending MIAM, starting mediation, and making child arrangements DURING the 20-week divorce reflection period — is not presented anywhere as a coherent timeline.

## 2. Official guidance gaps (GOV.UK, MoneyHelper, Citizens Advice)

### GOV.UK divorce
- Presents divorce as separate from finances and children with no clear linkage
- No warning about danger of finalising divorce without financial order
- Joint vs sole application strategic implications not explained
- No human-readable timeline or calendar-based explanation
- Help with Fees sequencing (apply BEFORE divorce) not made explicit

### GOV.UK financial orders
- Consent order page is misleading — implies D81 is all you need, doesn't explain you need a drafted consent order document
- Form E (28+ pages) linked as PDF with minimal guidance on how to complete it
- No guidance on when you need Form E vs D81
- Contested financial remedy process barely described
- No mention of pension complexity (CETVs, pension sharing orders, PODE)

### GOV.UK child arrangements
- No practical examples of what good arrangements look like
- Parenting Plan mentioned but no template provided
- MIAM requirement mentioned but exemptions not listed
- Child maintenance (CMS) vs child arrangements conflated
- Enforcement of orders poorly covered

### MoneyHelper
- Best financial guidance available but doesn't bridge to legal process
- Calculator is basic, doesn't handle complex assets
- No guidance on when you need a solicitor vs DIY
- Free pension appointment poorly signposted

### Citizens Advice
- Warmest and most accessible but lacks depth
- Legal aid cliff edge acknowledged but no practical alternative offered
- Child maintenance vs child arrangements confusion persists

## 3. Real user pain points (Mumsnet, Reddit, forums)

### "Where do I even start?"
Overwhelmingly the #1 question. Thread titles: "How to START divorce process", "Where do I start?", "I'm lost, need advice on divorce", "Lost".

### Financial confusion dominates
- "How the frig do I do the Form E?"
- "What happens if he refuses financial disclosure?"
- "Did you go for his pension?"
- "How do I afford to give H the equity in the house?"
- Form E described as terrifying, 29 pages, requiring 12 months bank statements

### Pension confusion is universal
- "I had no idea mine would be worth that much!!" (NHS pension)
- CETV vs actual retirement value not understood
- Defined benefit pensions (NHS, teachers) consistently undervalued by CETV
- Users don't know they need a PODE (Pension on Divorce Expert)

### Emotional register
- "Completely broken", "emotionally destroyed and lost", "scared to go to sleep"
- Divorce described as "grief and loss and bereavement"
- "An overwhelm and flood of emotions all the time"
- Panic attacks triggered by solicitor letters
- Average time to "acceptance" cited as approximately four years

### Privacy and secret planning
- Multiple Mumsnet threads about planning to leave without partner knowing
- "Planning to leave husband in secret", "AIBU to be planning on leaving my DH without him knowing"
- People in abusive/controlling relationships NEED to plan secretly for safety
- Concerns about solicitor letters arriving at shared homes
- Gathering financial documents before partner can hide assets

### What people wish they'd known
- Get a solicitor earlier
- Don't accept bad terms for a quiet life
- Act quickly, don't give partner time to prepare
- Get counselling
- Protect finances early
- Understand pensions properly
- It's three processes, not one
- The financial settlement is what takes the time
- It gets better: "My life started when I got divorced"

### What people get wrong
1. "Divorce is one thing" (it's three)
2. "We can just agree and that's legally binding" (need consent order)
3. "50/50 is automatic" (no, courts consider Section 25 factors)
4. "Mothers always get custody" (no presumption; "custody" isn't even the legal term)
5. "I don't need to disclose my pension" (you do)
6. "CETV = what my pension is worth" (often not, especially defined benefit)
7. "Mediation is compulsory" (MIAM is, mediation itself isn't)
8. "I can do my own consent order" (courts frequently reject self-drafted ones)

### Costs — what people report
- DIY divorce: £612 (court fee only)
- Consent order drafting: £400–£3,000
- Amicable with solicitor: ~£10,000 total
- Contested: £20,000–£40,000+
- Mediation: ~£500/session, average total £3,000–3,500 shared
- Government mediation voucher: up to £500 (poorly known)

## 4. Practitioner findings

### Most common early mistakes (per solicitors)
1. Not gathering financial information early enough
2. Taking legal advice from friends/family instead of professionals
3. Posting on social media
4. Assuming divorce ends financial ties
5. Leaving the family home without legal advice
6. Not understanding costs and timescales
7. Delaying pension advice (CETVs take months)
8. Remarrying without a financial order

### Resolution process options spectrum
DIY → Mediation → Hybrid mediation → Collaborative law → Resolution Together → Solicitor negotiation → Arbitration → Court

### Pension pitfalls
- Offsetting (trading pension for house equity) is loaded with risk
- CETV does NOT reflect true income value, especially defined benefit schemes
- Family Justice Council 2024 guidance strongly encourages PODE reports early
- Pre-marriage pensions can become "matrimonialised"
- State pension impact often forgotten entirely
- Women at particular risk of pension poverty after divorce

### Form E practitioner tips
- Start early — document gathering takes weeks/months
- Work in sections over several days
- Follow the grey boxes listing required supporting documents
- Check arithmetic, label attachments clearly
- Everything must be disclosed — lying is perjury

### Key misconceptions practitioners correct
- "Common law marriage" — 79% of people believe it exists. It doesn't in England and Wales.
- Only 2% of divorces reach contested final hearing
- "Quickie divorce" — 74% think it exists. Minimum 26 weeks, average 44 weeks.

## 5. Safeguarding and domestic abuse findings

### Separation is the most dangerous time
- 41% of women killed by partners/former partners had separated or taken steps to
- 30% died within the first month of separation
- 70% killed within the first year
- 95% of domestic abuse cases involve economic abuse

### Post-separation abuse
- Coercive control became criminal offence in 2015 (during relationship) and April 2023 (post-separation)
- Uses children as weapons, financial control, exploitation of court systems, surveillance
- Financial abuse can continue or START after separation
- The divorce process itself can be weaponised

### Mediation and domestic abuse
- Women's Aid opposes mandatory mediation in DA cases
- Harm Panel report found mediation can increase unequal power relations
- MIAM screening is not conclusive — mediators will not always detect abuse
- Shuttle mediation may be insufficient (intimidation before/after, control through mediator)
- Victims of coercive control cannot negotiate freely

### MIAM exemptions
15 exemption categories exist. Domestic abuse exemptions require evidence including: police records, court orders, health professional letters, domestic abuse support service letters, IDVA letters.

### FMC safeguarding standards
- Individual screening mandatory at every MIAM and ongoing throughout mediation
- Screening covers: domestic abuse, power imbalances, incapacity, child protection
- FMC developing new standardised screening tool (2025 initiative)
- DASH risk checklist: 27 questions covering DA, stalking, "honour"-based violence

### Digital safety
- 72% of women accessing Refuge services reported tech-facilitated abuse
- Stalkerware, location tracking, financial surveillance, hidden cameras
- GOV.UK Design System has an official "Exit this page" component
- Redirect to BBC Weather (neutral page), keyboard shortcut (Shift x3)
- Service must warn about browser history, shared devices, notification risks

## 6. Competitor landscape

### Key finding: no unified workspace exists

Every product addresses one slice. Nobody offers a single environment covering the entire journey.

| Product | What it does | What it doesn't do |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Amicable** | End-to-end service (£300–£5,910), strong brand, Trustpilot 5 stars | Not a workspace — you pay people. No Form E tool. Requires cooperative partner. Not SRA-regulated |
| **SeparateSpace** | Personalised pathway, guides/templates, AI coach (£25/mo) | No interactive financial tools, no Form E helper, no shared workspace, single-user only |
| **OurFamilyWizard** | Co-parenting communication, expense tracking, ToneMeter AI | Post-separation only. No divorce/financial/disclosure help. Both parents need paid subs |
| **Splitifi** | AI platform with Form E builder, calculators | US-origin, USD pricing, unproven in UK |
| **Divorce-Online** | Form filing, consent orders (£399–£649) | Pure admin, no guidance or workspace |
| **Relate Planner** | Free readiness assessment, calculators | Purely informational, no workflow |

### The gap our product fills

1. No consumer-friendly Form E tool exists
2. No shared workspace between separating parties
3. No applicant-first, workspace-led product
4. No financial disentanglement workspace
5. No wellbeing integrated with practical tools
6. The "squeezed middle" (can't afford solicitors, need more than form-filing) is underserved
7. No document management for separation at consumer level
